History of dust.ess.uci.edu

The original Zender group server, dust.ess.uci.edu, was born in September, 1999, for about $10k. Dell provided a Precision 610 dual Intel Pentium 500 MHz, 1 GB RAM, with 2×36 GB SCSI disks. One shot disk, one fried video card, and almost five years later, her 1920×1200 resolution monitor still weighs about ninety-pounds and is as good as it gets. dust's disks whined like spoiled babies, yet they never failed until one week when I was really busy, but that's another story. She originally ran Apache 1.x on factory RedHat Linux 6.0. Now she runs Apache 2.0.40 on RedHat Linux 9. The physical machine dust was retired from active server duty 20041228, when it was replaced by sand which was renamed dust. The original dust continued to run for many years in retirement under the alias dirt.ess.uci.edu.

From 20041228—20090206 the group server dust.ess.uci.edu was the physical machine sand.ess.uci.edu. The hostname dust.ess.uci.edu became a “virtual” name (via a designated IP pointer) on 20050105, and has remained virtual ever since. Requests to dust are automatically routed to the current real server. Using the dust address for URLs and web services provides continuity with sites that link to dust and eliminates the need to edit web pages everytime we changes physical web servers.

sand was a 64-bit AMD GNU/Linux system—complete independence from the Wintel monopolies at last! Western Scientific sold me a dual AMD Opteron Processor 244 (1.8 MHz); 2 GB RAM, with 2×250 GB IDE disks for about $4000. The disks were mirrored to provide robust data storage. Her original fans were noisy, like small jet engines under my desk. Western Scientific replaced those with quiet fans and now I'm happy. She originally ran Apache 1.x on factory SuSE Linux Professional version 9.0. She last ran Apache 2.0.50 under Ubuntu's 64-bit Debian-based GNU/Linux distribution. Harry Mangalam installed a very fast eight disk RAID5 with 2 TB capacity. In February, 2009, sand returned to private life and the group server became pbs.

pbs.ess.uci.edu became the group server on the afternoon of Friday, February 6, 2009. By that evening she had crashed and all data was completely inaccessible for about a week. This after weeks and months planning and crafting her as our first rack-mounted, uninterruptible, access-controlled server. Sometimes less planning is better.

pbs was originally a 64-bit AMD GNU/Linux system running Ubuntu 8.10, Intrepid Ibex. It was the head node of the former cluster named pbs, and sat in CalIT2's server room in dedicated rack eventually donated by my group to CalIT2. pbs began life as the development cluster for the NCO/SDO project. Daniel Wang used pbs extensively while he wrote his thesis and developed SWAMP.

Western Scientific sold us the four-node pbs cluster for about $40000 in 2006. Each node had 16 GB RAM, inifiniband fabric, and about 1 TB of disk. When we reconfigured the cluster into a server, we combined into 2.5 TB of RAID level fxm for pbs. The other three nodes pbs1, pbs2, and pbs3, are now used as a standalone servers and backup destinations. A daily cron job uses rsync to back-up /home and /data/www from pbs to pbs1.

On November 22, 2011, dust was pointed to another machine reconfigured as pbs. This was a pure Apache2 web server based on a 2U Broadcom retiree with dual Opterons and a 1.5 TB mdadm RAID5. It last ran Ubuntu Lucid 10.04.3 LTS. /home, was a single OS disk, mirrored nightly to the RAID5. The previous pbs became pbs4, a storage server for the BDUC cluster running gluster over RDMA/Infiniband. The new pbs ran for about four years until failing in about May 2014.

Memory of the server configuration between May 2014 and October 2015 has been lost. Perhaps there was no dust during this period, which was difficult emotionally and financially.

On October 12, 2015, firn.ess.uci.edu became dust. firn is a MacBook Pro with 16 GB RAM that originally ran OS X 10.11, Mountain Lion, and now runs MacOS 11.2, Big Sur. firn is truly portable, and during emergencies serves as my personal workstation. During such a period in late 2018, triggered by a coffee spill, I noticed that the casing of firn was warped, presumably a symptom of being constantly on, and sometimes pegged. Nevertheless, firn has continued to serve admirably for six years. The cheap, third party RJ45<->USB adapters that I buy to connect firn's USB2 port to the Internet require manual upgrading of the device drivers after every OS upgrade because Apple keeps its hardware interface logic proprietary. Because the third party adapters did not keep up with the Catalina-to-Big Sur transition, the Dust's static IP and webserver were offline from December, 2020 until March, 2021.
